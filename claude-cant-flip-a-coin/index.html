Claude Can't Flip a Coin

March 2026

Claude says heads. Every time.

200 / 0
heads / tails, across 200 independent API calls — each one a fresh request, no memory, no conversation

LLMs don't flip coins. They predict the next token. The model isn't choosing between heads and tails—it's answering a different question: what would a helpful AI assistant most likely say here?

I tested ten different prompts trying to fix it. A system prompt insisting it be fair. Listing tails first. Asking for just H or T instead of the full words. Injecting a random string from Math.random() into each prompt to perturb the attention pattern. Most of them barely moved the needle.

Then I tried asking for twenty flips at once instead of one.

Fifty API calls, twenty flips each, one thousand total.

502 / 498
heads / tails, across 1,000 flips in batches of 20 (p = 0.69)

Statistically indistinguishable from a fair coin. Five of the sequences:

Heads Tails
Per-sequence P(Heads): mean = 0.502, std = 0.028

But look more carefully. Every sequence has roughly 10 heads and 10 tails. The longest streak in any of them is three. In truly random 20-flip sequences, 77% should contain a run of four or more, and 47% should have a run of five or more. The model produces none.

The standard deviation of the heads ratio across our 50 sequences is 0.028. For true randomness it should be 0.112—four times higher.

There's a well-known result in psychology: when humans try to write down a "random" sequence, they over-alternate and under-repeat. Streaks feel non-random even though they're statistically expected, so people avoid them. The model learned from those people. It inherited our folk theory of randomness—balanced, streak-averse, neat—rather than randomness itself.

The batch prompt got us to 502/498 because we described what frequency should look like, and the model matched it. We said nothing about streaks, so it fell back on its training data—which fell back on human intuition—which avoids them. We got exactly what we asked for. Everything else was filled in for us.

Every part of the output we don't constrain is filled in by defaults we don't notice.

Ask Claude to flip a coin and it says heads. Ask it to fake a sequence and it fakes one that looks right to both of you. The question is what else it's faking that you haven't thought to check.

All experiments used Claude Sonnet 4 (claude-sonnet-4-20250514) at temperature 1.0. Each API call was a single-turn message with no system prompt unless noted. Independence was verified by checking that every response carried a unique message ID. Statistical significance was assessed with a two-tailed binomial test (normal approximation, α = 0.05). Total: ~1,350 API calls.
